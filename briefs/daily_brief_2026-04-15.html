Hungarian PM Viktor Orbán Defeated After 16 Years, Péter Magyar Wins Landslide Election

Al Jazeera English

Hungarian Prime Minister Viktor Orbán conceded defeat after his Fidesz party lost in a landslide to Péter Magyar's Tisza party, which secured 138 seats in the 199-seat parliament with 53.6% of the vote. The election ended Orbán's 16-year grip on power and his increasingly authoritarian rule. Magyar, a former Orbán loyalist who left Fidesz in 2024, campaigned on anti-corruption and promised to rebuild Hungary's relationships with the EU and NATO.

Why it matters: This election reshapes European politics by removing Putin's main EU ally and Trump's closest European partner, potentially unblocking EU aid to Ukraine and strengthening European unity.

Additional: NPR

President Trump announced a naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz after negotiations between the US and Iran in Islamabad collapsed without a deal. The US military said it would blockade ships entering or exiting Iranian ports, with Vice President JD Vance having led the unsuccessful talks. Why it matters: The blockade of one of the world's most critical shipping routes threatens global oil supplies and food security, with UN officials warning of potential hunger catastrophe from disrupted fertilizer and commodity trade.

WHO Reports Surge in Antibiotic-Resistant Infections Across Southeast Asia

Additional: STAT News

The World Health Organization issued a warning about a 40% increase in antibiotic-resistant bacterial infections across Southeast Asia over the past 18 months, with particular concern about carbapenem-resistant Enterobacteriaceae. The surge is attributed to overuse of antibiotics during and after the COVID-19 pandemic, combined with inadequate infection control in healthcare settings.

Why it matters: Rising antibiotic resistance threatens to make common infections untreatable and could reverse decades of medical progress, with the WHO estimating 10 million annual deaths from resistant infections by 2050 if current trends continue.

Why it matters: The findings reveal an unexpected health consequence of rising global temperatures, suggesting climate change's medical impacts extend far beyond heat stroke and respiratory conditions.
